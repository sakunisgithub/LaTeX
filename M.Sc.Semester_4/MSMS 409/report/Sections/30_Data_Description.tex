\section{Data Description}

The datasets used in this project consist of historical financial time series data from two major stock market indices, namely the NIFTY50 index of India and the SSE50 index of China. These indices are widely regarded as important indicators of the economic and financial performance of their respective markets. The NIFTY50 index represents the weighted performance of fifty leading companies listed on the National Stock Exchange of India, while the SSE50 index tracks fifty large scale and highly liquid companies listed on the Shanghai Stock Exchange. Since both indices reflect the behaviour of major financial markets, they provide suitable benchmark datasets for evaluating stochastic forecasting models.

The historical data were collected over multiple years with observations recorded on trading days. Each observation primarily consists of the daily closing prices of the corresponding index. Financial time series data generally exhibit non-stationary behaviour characterized by trends, volatility clustering and sudden fluctuations. Such properties make these datasets appropriate for studying stochastic neural differential equation models which are specifically designed to capture uncertainty and nonlinear temporal dynamics.

For the forecasting task, the data were transformed into sequential input-output pairs suitable for supervised learning frameworks. Sliding window techniques were employed to construct the input sequences, where a fixed number of past observations were utilized to predict future index values over different forecasting horizons. In this project, prediction horizons of 1, 3, 7 and 14 days were considered in order to evaluate both short term and relatively longer term forecasting performance. This setup allows analysis of how prediction accuracy changes as the forecasting horizon increases.

Normalization techniques were applied to the financial series before feeding them into the neural network models in order to improve numerical stability and facilitate efficient optimization. The datasets were subsequently divided into training and testing subsets based on chronological order, ensuring that the temporal dependency structure of the series was preserved. Such a division reflects realistic forecasting conditions in financial applications, where future information is unavailable during the training stage.

The use of financial datasets from two different markets also allows comparative analysis of the robustness and adaptability of stochastic differential equation based neural networks under varying market conditions. Since both indices contain complex nonlinear and stochastic patterns, they provide an effective framework for studying the capability of Brownian motion driven SDE-Net models and Lévy driven differential equation models in capturing uncertainty and forecasting future market behaviour.
